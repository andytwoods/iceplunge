# Cold Cognition Project — Overview

A community-led citizen-science platform that captures repeated cognitive measurements
from cold-water immersion practitioners before and after plunges, building a longitudinal
dataset to study the acute and chronic effects of cold-water immersion on cognition.

---

## Cognitive task battery

Each session presents five tasks in a randomised order
(`CognitiveSession.task_order`, seeded for reproducibility).

| Key | Name | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| `pvt` | Psychomotor Vigilance Task | ~60 s |
| `sart` | Sustained Attention to Response Task | ~75 s |
| `flanker` | Eriksen Flanker Task | ~75 s |
| `digit_symbol` | Digit Symbol Coding | ~75 s |
| `mood` | Mood Rating | self-paced |

Task metadata (labels, durations, instructions) lives in `iceplunge/tasks/registry.py`.
Task JS runners live in `iceplunge/static/tasks/js/`.

### Session flow

```
tasks:start  (covariate form)
     ↓
tasks:hub    (task list + instructions + "Start next task" button)
     ↓
tasks:task   (full-screen task runner — no navbar/footer)
     ↓  (on complete or skip)
tasks:hub    (updated status — repeat until all tasks done)
     ↓
tasks:complete
```

A practice/try mode (`tasks:try/<task_type>/`) creates an `is_practice=True` session and
shows a prominent banner; `task_core.js` detects `window.TASK_IS_PRACTICE` and shows a
SweetAlert2 "Practice complete" dialog instead of navigating away. Practice sessions are
stored but excluded from all analyses.

## Mood Rating task — scale design rationale

The Mood Rating task uses **four 7-point bipolar scales**. The choice of scale points and
endpoint anchors is grounded in published psychometric instruments.

### Why 7 points?

Seven-point scales are the standard format for bipolar rating in affect research (cf.
Russell et al., 1989; Osgood et al., 1957 semantic differential tradition). Compared with
5-point scales, 7-point scales provide greater discriminability and are more sensitive to
within-person change over time — important for detecting acute post-plunge effects.
Compared with 9- or 11-point scales, 7 points reduces response burden in ecological
momentary assessment (EMA) contexts where scales are completed repeatedly.

### Anchor words and their sources

#### Mood (valence)
- **Low anchor (1):** "Very bad"
- **High anchor (7):** "Very good"
- **Source:** Feeling Scale (FS; Hardy & Rejeski, 1989). The FS is the most widely used
  and cited single-item valence measure in exercise and health research. Its native
  11-point (−5 to +5) format includes labeled anchors "Very bad" / "Very good" at the
  poles; the labeled points map directly onto a 7-point compression.
- **Reference:** Hardy, C. J., & Rejeski, W. J. (1989). Not what, but how one feels:
  The measurement of affect during exercise. *Journal of Sport and Exercise Psychology,
  11*(3), 304–317.

#### Energy (arousal)
- **Low anchor (1):** "Calm / low energy"
- **High anchor (7):** "Energetic / excited"
- **Source:** Felt Arousal Scale (FAS; Svebak & Murgatroyd, 1985), corroborated by the
  Self-Assessment Manikin (SAM; Bradley & Lang, 1994). The FAS is a 6-point unipolar
  scale (1 = "Low arousal", 6 = "High arousal"); the SAM arousal dimension runs from
  "Calm / relaxed" to "Excited / stimulated" on a 9-point pictographic scale. Both
  instruments anchor the same subjective poles. The combined wording "Calm / low energy"
  and "Energetic / excited" captures both the activation and hedonic dimensions of the
  construct and aligns with Thayer's (1989) Energetic Arousal dimension of the
  AD-ACL (tired ↔ energetic/awake).
- **References:**
  - Svebak, S., & Murgatroyd, S. (1985). Metamotivational dominance: A multimethod
    validation of reversal theory constructs. *Journal of Personality and Social
    Psychology, 48*(1), 107–116.
  - Bradley, M. M., & Lang, P. J. (1994). Measuring emotion: The self-assessment manikin
    and the semantic differential. *Journal of Behavior Therapy and Experimental
    Psychiatry, 25*(1), 49–59.
  - Thayer, R. E. (1989). *The Biopsychology of Mood and Arousal.* Oxford University Press.

#### Stress
- **Low anchor (1):** "Not at all stressed"
- **High anchor (7):** "Extremely stressed"
- **Source:** EMA single-item stress literature and the Stress Numerical Rating Scale 11
  (Stress NRS-11; Karvounides et al., 2016). The NRS-11 is an 11-point (0–10) single-item
  scale validated against the STAI, modelled on the Pain NRS-11 and widely used in EMA
  research. Compressing to 7 points retains the validated anchor wording "no stress" /
  "extreme stress". The Bristol SHAW single-item job-stress measure uses identical extreme
  anchors ("not at all stressful" / "extremely stressful") on a 5-point scale, confirming
  the cross-instrument consistency of this wording.
- **Reference:** Karvounides, D., Simpson, P., et al. (2016). Three studies supporting
  the initial validation of the Stress NRS-11: A single-item measure of momentary stress
  for adolescents and adults. *OA Text.*

#### Mental clarity (sharpness)
- **Low anchor (1):** "Mentally foggy"
- **High anchor (7):** "Mentally sharp"
- **Source:** Stanford Sleepiness Scale (SSS; Hoddes et al., 1973) — the only major
  validated single-item scale that is natively 7-point in this domain. The SSS uses
  ordered verbal descriptors running from "Feeling active, vital, alert, or wide awake"
  (1) through "Somewhat foggy, let down" (4) to "No longer fighting sleep" (7). The
  SSS direction is inverted here (foggy = low, sharp = high) to match the positive-high
  convention of the other three scales. The anchor words "foggy" and "alert / sharp"
  are taken directly from SSS descriptor language. Convergent validity with the 9-point
  Karolinska Sleepiness Scale (r = 0.742) further supports the construct validity of
  these anchors.
- **Reference:** Hoddes, E., Zarcone, V., Smythe, H., Phillips, R., & Dement, W. C.
  (1973). Quantification of sleepiness: A new approach. *Psychophysiology, 10*(4),
  431–436.

## Key implementation notes

- `device_meta` (JSONField on `CognitiveSession`) stores ad-hoc per-session metadata
  including `skipped_tasks` (list of task type strings) and `interruption_logs`.
  No migrations are needed to add new keys.
- Module-level settings such as `MAX_VOLUNTARY_SESSIONS_PER_HOUR` in
  `iceplunge/tasks/helpers/rate_limits.py` are read at import time; patch via
  `monkeypatch.setattr(module, "VAR", val)` in tests, not via `settings`.
- All tests require a `ConsentProfile` to pass `ConsentRequiredMiddleware`; use the
  `_consented_user()` and `_superuser()` test helpers.
